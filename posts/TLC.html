Generic Trade Life Cycle

In the world of financial trading, every transaction follows a detailed process, ensuring compliance, risk management, and efficient execution. Whether you're a seasoned investor or just starting to explore the intricacies of financial markets, understanding the trade life cycle is essential. In this post, we’ll break down the key steps involved, from client onboarding to post-settlement reporting, providing a comprehensive look at how trades are processed in a financial institution.

Client Onboarding & Registration

Before any trading takes place, it's crucial to onboard the client properly. This involves collecting various documents and conducting thorough checks to comply with regulatory standards, especially anti-money laundering (AML) rules.

  1. Documentation Collection:
    • Memorandum and Articles of Association
    • ISDA Master Agreements (International Swaps and Derivatives Association)
    • Signatures from company directors
    • Evidence of the nature of business
  2. Know Your Customer (KYC) Process: The KYC process helps ensure that the institution knows exactly who it is trading with and is in full compliance with AML regulations.
  3. Establishing Credit Limits: Before proceeding, it’s vital to ensure that the client has sufficient credit limits in place, allowing for risk management and smoother transaction execution.

The Generic Trade Life Cycle

Once the client is onboarded and ready to trade, the actual process of executing a trade unfolds in several steps. Below is an overview of the generic trade life cycle:

1. Order Placement (Front Office)

The process begins when an investor decides to buy or sell a security. They approach a broker, who then places the order on their behalf.

2. Trade Initiation

During trade initiation, the trader negotiates and discusses key aspects of the trade with the counterparty. This includes deciding the notional amount, interest rates (for instruments like IRS), risk premium, payment schedules, and the terms of the contract.

3. Trade Execution (Front Office)

Once the order is placed, the broker matches the trade in the market. When the order is matched, the trade is executed, and a trade ticket is created.

4. Trade Capture/Booking (Front Office)

After execution, the trade is booked into internal systems. Key economic details such as the quantity, price, ISIN, trade date, and payment dates are captured to ensure proper processing.

5. Trade Capture/Booking (Middle Office)

Once the trade details are captured in the front office, they are transferred to the middle office, which further updates the risk management and settlement systems.

6. Trade Enrichment (Middle Office)

At this stage, additional information is added to the trade, including non-economic details like SSI (Standard Settlement Instructions), instrument identifiers, and exchange details.

7. Trade Allocation

The next step is to allocate the executed trade to various client portfolios or accounts. A single trade might be split across different clients or fund portfolios based on the fund’s management strategy.

8. Trade Verification (Middle Office)

Here, the middle office team verifies the details of the trade to ensure that everything matches the original terms agreed upon and no errors were made during the process.

9. Trade Validation/Amendment (Middle Office)

During this stage, any discrepancies or amendments to the trade, such as changes in quantity, price, or commission, are corrected. This ensures that the trade remains valid and accurate in the system.

10. Position/P&L Control (Middle Office)

The middle office regularly checks the profit and loss (P&L) for each trading book to ensure the trader is working from the most up-to-date and accurate positions.

11. Pre-Confirmation/Agree on SSIs

To reduce settlement risks, the middle office ensures that all key economic details and SSIs are confirmed with the counterparty before settlement takes place. This helps prevent payment errors and ensures the funds go to the correct counterparty.

12. Trade Agreement & Affirmation

The trade agreement is the process where both counterparties agree to the terms of the trade. This may be done through verbal affirmation or written confirmation.

13. Transaction Reporting (Middle Office)

After the trade is confirmed, it must be reported to the relevant regulators. This ensures transparency and compliance with market regulations.

14. Pre-Settlement (Middle Office)

At this point, the middle office checks the collateral or securities needed to settle the trade. Ensuring that all conditions are met reduces the risk of settlement failure.

15. Settlement (Middle Office)

The trade is now settled, meaning that the securities are exchanged between counterparties. At this stage, both parties fulfill their obligations under the contract.

16. Post-Settlement (Back Office)

Once the trade is settled, the back office handles reconciliation and net asset value (NAV) calculations. The back office is also responsible for handling failed trades, interest claims, and other post-settlement activities.

17. Performance Reporting (Back Office/Front Office)

Finally, the back office provides performance reports to the front office, which uses these reports to analyze the success of the trade and to attract more investors.

Conclusion

Understanding the trade life cycle is essential for anyone involved in financial trading, from institutional investors to retail traders. It involves multiple departments working in tandem to ensure that trades are executed correctly, managed efficiently, and settled accurately. By following each step carefully, financial institutions can minimize risk, ensure regulatory compliance, and provide better service to their clients.

Whether you’re new to trading or an experienced investor, familiarizing yourself with these processes can help you navigate the complexities of the financial markets more effectively.
